## Cross-track rules

- **Specificity beats breadth**: one sharp niche beats fifteen technologies.
- **Proof in the same sentence**: years, scale, or named outcomes when possible.
- **Opinions**: what do you believe that others in your niche would debate?
- Avoid geography-first headlines unless the user explicitly targets local-only roles.

## What to avoid in positioning statements

| Pattern | Problem |
|---------|---------|
| "Passionate about technology" | Meaningless — everyone says this |
| "Full-stack" when one track is clearly the strength | Dilutes the positioning |
| Listing every technology known | No differentiation — reads as skill inventory |
| Future tense ("looking to grow into...") | Projects uncertainty, not authority |
| Geography-first ("Based in São Paulo") | Limits perception for remote-first international roles |

## The opinion test

Strong personal brands are built on specific opinions.

Ask: *What do I believe that most senior engineers in my niche would disagree with or hasn't been said clearly yet?*

Opinions become content. Content builds credibility. Credibility attracts the right opportunities.
